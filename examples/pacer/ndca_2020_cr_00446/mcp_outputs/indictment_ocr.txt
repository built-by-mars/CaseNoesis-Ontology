United States Export Statutes and Regulations

7. Under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (“IEEPA”), 50 U.S.C.

§§ 1701-07, the President of the United States was granted authority to deal with unusual and
extraordinary threats to the national security and foreign policy of the United States. Under IEEPA, the
President could declare a national emergency through Executive Orders that have the full force and
effect of law.

8. IEEPA also empowered the U.S. Department of Commerce (“DOC’’) to issue regulations
governing exports. Initially, the Export Administration Act (“EAA”), 50 App. U.S.C. §§ 2401-2420,
regulated the export of goods, technology, and software from the United States. Pursuant to the
provisions of the EAA, the DOC promulgated the Export Administration Regulations (“EAR”), 15
C.F.R. §§ 730-774, which contain additional restrictions on the export of goods outside of the United
States, consistent with the policies and provisions of the EAA. See 15 C.F.R. § 730.02. Although the
EAA lapsed on August 17, 2001, pursuant to the authority provided to the President under IEEPA, the
President issued Executive Order 13222, which declared a national emergency with respect to the
unusual and extraordinary threat to the national security, foreign policy, and economy of the United
States in light of the expiration of the EAA. Accordingly, pursuant to IEEPA, the President ordered that
the EAR’s provisions remain in full force and effect despite the expiration of the EAA. Presidents have

issued annual Executive Notices extending the national emergency declared in Executive Order 13222

from the time period covered by that Executive Order. Under IEEPA, it is a crime to willfully violate
any regulation promulgated thereunder, including the EAR. See 50 U.S.C. § 1705.

9. Pursuant to its authority derived from IEEPA, the DOC reviewed and controlled the
export of certain items, including goods, software, and technologies, from the United States to foreign
countries through the EAR. In particular, the EAR restricted the export of items that could make a
significant contribution to the military potential of other nations or that could be detrimental to the
foreign policy or national security of the United States. The EAR imposed licensing and other
requirements for items subject to the EAR to be lawfully exported from the United States or lawfully re-
exported from one foreign destination to another. The most sensitive items subject to EAR controls
were identified on the Commerce Control List, or “CCL,” published at 15 C.F.R. Part 774, Supp. No. 1.
Items on the CCL were categorized by Export Control Classification Number (“ECCN”), each of which
has export controls requirements depending on destination, end use, and end user. Those items subject
to the EAR but not specified on the CCL are identified by an “EAR99” designation.

10. The EAR further imposed, pursuant to the “Entity List” published at 15 C.F.R. § 774,
Supp. No. 4, licensing and other requirements for sales to certain companies, businesses, research
institutions, government and private organizations, individuals, and other types of legal persons that are
subject to specific license requirements for the export, re-export and/or transfer (in-country) of specified
items. Ifa license is required for a transaction, a U.S. party to the transaction must submit an export
license application prior to export.

11. On August 13, 2018, the President signed into law the National Defense Authorization
Act of 2019, which includes provisions on export controls, entitled the Export Control Reform Act of
2018 (““ECRA”), 50 U.S.C. §§ 4801-4852. In part, ECRA provides permanent statutory authority for the
EAR.

required to be filed with the United States government in connection with export shipments from the

end-user or ultimate destination of the export, the contents of the shipment including any control
numbers, and the value of the contents. The filing of an SED is completed either in paper or through the
submission of Electronic Export Information (“EEI’’) via the Automated Export System (“AES”). AES

Protection (“CBP”). SEDs and EEIs are official documents submitted to DHS in connection with export
shipments from the United States.

13. The DOC required the filing of electronic EEIs through the AES pursuant to Title 13,
United States Code, Section 305 and Foreign Trade Regulations (“FTR”’), Title 15, Code of Federal
Regulations, Part 30. The purpose of these requirements was to strengthen the United States
government’s ability to prevent the export of certain items to unauthorized destinations and end users
because the AES aids in targeting, identifying, and, when necessary, confiscating suspicious or illegal
shipments prior to exportation. 15 C.F.R. § 30.1(b).

14. The EEI contains statements to the United States government that the transaction
occurred as described. The contents of the EEI are relied upon by the Department of State, the
Department of the Treasury, and the Department of Commerce for export control purposes. The EAR at
15 C.F.R. § 764.2(g) states that “No person may make any false or misleading representation, statement,
or certification, or falsify or conceal any material fact, either directly to the Department of Commerce,
Bureau of Industry and Security (“BIS”), the United States Customs Service, or an official of any other

COUNT ONE: (50 U.S.C. § 1705 and 15 C.F.R. Parts 764.2(d) — Conspiracy to Violate IEEPA)

19. Paragraphs | through 18 above are incorporated and realleged as if fully set forth here.

20. Beginning at a date unknown to the Grand Jury, but no later than May 13, 2015, and
continuing through a date unknown to the Grand Jury, but to at least August 13, 2018, in the Northern
District of California and elsewhere, the defendants,

HAN LI, and
LIN CHEN,
and others known and unknown to the Grand Jury, did knowingly and willfully combine, conspire,
confederate, and agree to export, reexport, and supply, and cause to be exported, reexported, and
supplied, directly and indirectly, goods and services from the United States to Chengdu GaStone
Technology Co. and other end users in the People’s Republic of China, without a license and in
violation of the United States Department of Commerce Entity List published at 15 C.F.R. § 774, Supp.
No. 4.
OBJECTS OF THE CONSPIRACY

21. The objects of the conspiracy were: to purchase items from the United States, and cause
those items to be shipped to entities and research institutions in the PRC listed on the DOC Entity List
and prohibited from receiving such controlled items, through intermediary companies such as JHI for
purposes of concealing the nature of the exports and true end users, in violation of the prohibitions and
licensing requirements.
