Interstate Nexus

The government can prove that a defendant knew or had reason to know that a visual depiction

5 would be mailed or transported across state lines or in foreign commerce. Alternatively, the government
6 can prove that a visual depiction was actually transported across state lines or in foreign commerce or
7 that it was produced using materials that had been mailed, shipped, or transported across state lines or in
8 foreign commerce. Presenting evidence of an electronic device’s “country-of-origin labels,” which
9 indicate that a device had been manufactured overseas, is sufficient in the Ninth Circuit “to prove that
10 the[y] moved in foreign commerce.” United States v. Fox, 357 Fed. Appx. 64, 65 (9th Cir. 2009) (citing
11 United States v. Patterson, 820 F.2d 1524, 1526 (9th Cir. 1987).

F.

404(b) Evidence Notice

14

The Ninth Circuit has recognized two categories of evidence that may be considered

15 “inextricably intertwined” with a charged offense and therefore admitted without regard to Federal Rule
16 of Evidence 404(b). See United States v. Vizcarra–Martinez, 66 F.3d 1006, 1012 (9th Cir.1995).
17 First, evidence of prior acts may be admitted if the evidence “constitutes a part of the transaction that
18 serves as the basis for the criminal charge.” Id. Second, prior act evidence may be admitted “when it was
19 necessary to do so in order to permit the prosecutor to offer a coherent and comprehensible story
20 regarding the commission of the crime.” Id. at 1012–13. The evidence that is referenced in this notice
21 fits within both categories. United States v. DeGeorge, 380 F.3d 1203, 1220 (9th Cir. 2004).
22

The United States has charged Gastelo with the actual and attempted sexual exploitation of a minor

23 (Count One) as well as the receipt or distribution of at least one image of a minor engaged in sexually
24 explicit conduct (Count Two). The indictment alleges that the crimes were committed within the date
25 range beginning no later than approximately January 2019 and continuing through approximately May
26 2020. During this time, Gastelo posed as an eighteen-year-old on the dating application Grindr. As
27 referenced above, he sent and received sexually explicit images utilizing this catfish account. Although
28 the real ages of the users Gastelo communicated with are unknown due to the Grindr policy of stating

Alternatively, the evidence is admissible under FRE 404(b) to prove Gastelo’s intent, his

6 preparation and planning to commit the offenses alleged in Count One and Two, as well as his motive.
7 Under Rule 404(b), evidence of other acts is not admissible to prove character but may be admissible to
8 prove “motive, opportunity, intent, preparation, plan, knowledge, identity, absence of mistake, or lack of
9 accident.” Fed. R. Evid. 404(b). Evidence under Rule 404(b) may be admitted if: (1) the evidence tends
10 to prove a material point; (2) the other act is not too remote in time; (3) the evidence is sufficient to support
11 a finding that the defendant committed the other act; and (4) in cases like this one, where knowledge and
12 intent are at issue, the other act is similar to the offense charged. See United States v. Cox, 963 F.3d 915,
13 924 (9th Cir. 2020). Even if the evidence is admissible under Rule 404(b), it remains subject to the general
14 balancing test under Federal Rule of Evidence 403. Id. at 925.
15

The jury should be given evidence that shows Gastelo’s intent and motive to create child

16 pornography in the past, his lack of mistake in creating child pornography, as well as his intent to find
17 images of minors that support the offense charged in Count Two. See United States v. Johnson, 132 F.3d
18 1279, 1283 (9th Cir. 1997) (explaining “other acts” need not be identical to the conduct charged); see also
19 United States v. Boam, 2023 WL 3722904 at *2. And “Rule 404(b) is a rule of inclusion—not exclusion—
20 which references at least three categories of other ‘acts’ encompassing the inner workings of the mind:
21 motive, intent, and knowledge.” United States v. Wells, 879 F.3d 900, 928 (9th Cir. 2018) (citing Curtin
22 489 F.3d 93, 944 (9th Cir. 2007) (en banc) …).
23

FORFEITURE

The indictment includes a forfeiture allegation, and the United States has since specified its

14 intent to seek forfeiture of Gastelo’s residence and various electronic devices that he used to commit his
15 crimes. Dkt. Nos. 9, 71, 75. Thus, the United States’ forfeiture allegations reflect the instrumentalities
16 and facilitating property connected to Counts One and Two.
17

The procedures for proving the forfeiture allegation are set forth in Rule 32.2 of the Federal

18 Rules of Criminal Procedure. As the United States seeks to forfeit “specific property” used in the
19 defendant’s crimes, the Court must determine whether the “government has established the requisite
20 nexus between the property and the offense.” Fed. R. Crim. P. 32.2(b)(1). The United States must
21 prove this “nexus” by a preponderance of the evidence.
22

Because there will a jury trial for the criminal charges, the “court must determine before the jury

23 begins deliberating whether either party requests that the jury be retained to determine the forfeitability
24 of specific property if it returns a guilty verdict.” Fed. R. Crim. P. 32.2(b)(5). The Ninth Circuit has
25 held that Rule 32.2(b)(5) places an “affirmative duty on the court” to determine whether either the
26 United States or defendant requests a jury determination regarding forfeiture. Mancuso, 718 F.3d 780,
27 799 (9th Cir. 2013). The inquiry must occur “before the jury retires,” but, in practice, many district
28 courts initiate the inquiry when empaneling the jury “to avoid an inadvertent waiver of the right to a jury
